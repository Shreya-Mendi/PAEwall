SLIDE 2 — PROBLEM & MOTIVATION  (target 50s · ~125 words)
================================================================

"U.S. patent licensing and litigation is a three-billion-dollar-a-year
market -- and the core search task is still done by hand.

A patent attorney today reads the claims, guesses which companies might
infringe, hand-searches 10-K filings and product pages, and then builds
a claim chart line by line. It costs five hundred dollars an hour,
takes weeks per patent, and it's recall-limited -- the right defendant
is often missed entirely.

The people locked out of this are individual inventors, small patent
funds, university tech-transfer offices, and in-house counsel priced
out of fifty-thousand-dollar-a-year tools like Patlytics or IP.com.

What's missing in the commercial space is a public benchmark, a
faithfulness-grounded pipeline, and a red-team module that gives
both sides the same honest view of enforcement viability."

[Advance to Slide 3.]

================================================================
SLIDE 3 — APPROACH  (target 50s · ~125 words)
================================================================

"My approach has three pieces.

First, the benchmark: I built PAE-Bench -- five hundred and twenty-two
patent-defendant pairs from real federal litigation, across five
technology verticals. It's the first public cross-vertical
patent-to-product retrieval benchmark.

Second, the pipeline: four modules -- patent intake, retrieval,
claim-chart generation with faithfulness scoring, and a red-team
counter-argument layer.

Third, the models. I benchmarked three retrievers head-to-head.
BM25 -- just keyword search -- gets thirteen percent Recall-at-10.
TF-IDF plus logistic regression gets to twenty-four percent. And a
fine-tuned dual encoder -- PatentSBERTa for the patent side,
all-mpnet-base-v2 for product descriptions, trained with InfoNCE
contrastive loss and hard negatives -- hits thirty-two point eight
percent. That's a one-hundred-fifty-two percent lift over keyword search."

[Pause. Advance to Slide 4 — Live Demo.]

SLIDE 4 — LIVE DEMO  (target 90s · ~225 words + actions)
================================================================

"Let me show you how this works live."

[Switch to browser tab with app open.]
[Click the "Protect My Patent" card.]

"This is the infringement-discovery flow. I'm going to use a real
software patent -- US-2014-289857 -- on computer-virus protection
via email sandboxing."

[Click the US-2014289857-A1 sample button.]

"The system parses the claims, runs retrieval, and returns the top
five most-likely infringers ranked by semantic similarity."

[Scroll to Microsoft result — should be rank 1.]

"Number one: Microsoft, with a retrieval score of zero-point-eight-four.
That's Defender for Office 365 -- Safe Attachments. Number two:
Palo Alto Networks, WildFire. Then Symantec, Cisco Secure Email,
and Proofpoint."

[Click or scroll to expand Microsoft's claim chart.]

"For each candidate, we generate a faithfulness-scored claim chart.
Every limitation in the patent gets mapped to evidence from the
company's product documentation -- and a confidence score from zero
to one."

[Point at a row with faithfulness_score: 0.95.]

"This row scores 0.95 because Microsoft's own documentation explicitly
describes Safe Attachments opening email attachments in an isolated
sandbox."

[Scroll to the Red Team section.]

"And this is the red-team output -- non-infringement arguments,
invalidity risks, and an enforcement probability. For Microsoft,
point-six-one. That's the honest answer, not the sales pitch."

[Switch back to slides. Advance to Slide 5.]

SLIDE 5 — RESULTS & KEY FINDINGS  (target 50s · ~125 words)
================================================================

"Three takeaways.

One: fine-tuned dense retrieval is the right primitive. Plus-one-fifty-two
percent Recall-at-10 over BM25. Software vertical gets to fifty-three
percent on its own.

Two: classical ML actually wins on ranking precision -- MRR and nDCG.
The dual encoder finds more answers but is data-limited at forty-nine
test examples. Next lever is a cross-encoder re-ranker.

Three: my error analysis surfaced a data-coverage pathology --
four of the top-five failures are IP-holding entities like Valtrus
with empty 10-K product sections. That's a corpus gap, not a model failure.

Commercially: public benchmark plus faithfulness plus red-team is an
unoccupied wedge. Patlytics, IP.com, ClaimChart LLM -- none publish numbers.
And on ethics: every chart ships with a faithfulness score, every
retrieval ships with the defense's argument. The tool raises the cost
of frivolous assertion."
